The tl;dr

65% of respondents feel at least somewhat better about their jobs since AI arrived, with only 22% feeling worse. The average rating of 3.69 sits comfortably on the positive side of neutral.

What's better:

  • Expanded capabilities and skills. 28 people mentioned feeling like they can do more than before, learning new skills and taking on challenges that previously felt out of reach.
  • Faster execution and productivity. 26 respondents cited speed gains, getting work done in hours instead of days.
  • Automating tedious tasks. 18 people celebrated ditching repetitive work, freeing up time for higher-value activities.

What's worse:

  • Pressure and increased expectations. 15 mentions of the dark side of productivity: when you can do more, you're expected to do more.
  • Job security concerns. 12 people worried about AI making their roles obsolete or less valuable to employers.
  • Keeping up and learning fatigue. 11 respondents felt exhausted by the constant need to learn new tools and adapt to changing workflows.

One more thing: People at companies with 5,001+ employees rated their experience at 3.31, barely above neutral, compared to 4.0 at companies with 2-10 people. The bigger the company, the less enthusiastic people feel.

Patterns worth noting

The honeymoon effect is real. People with less than 1 year of tenure rate their AI experience at 4.0 (much better territory), matching the 11+ year veterans. But tenure groups in between show steadily declining enthusiasm: 3.71 for 1-2 years, 3.39 for 3-5 years, and just 3.12 for the 6-10 year group. The novelty might be wearing off faster than expected.

Founders love AI, middle managers don't. Founder and C-suite respondents rated their experience at 4.33, the highest of any group. VPs and Directors came in at 3.75, and ICs at 3.61. But the single Group PM/Manager respondent gave a rating of 1.0 (much worse), suggesting the squeeze of increased expectations hits hardest in the middle.

Small companies are having more fun. Companies with 2-10 people show an average rating of 4.0, compared to 3.31 at enterprises with 5,001+ employees. Every size bracket below 1,000 employees sits above 3.5, but once you hit the big leagues, enthusiasm drops to barely positive. Corporate AI adoption might come with more baggage than opportunity.

The positives outweigh the negatives by volume. The top three positive themes (expanded capabilities, faster execution, automating tedious tasks) account for 72 total mentions. The top three negative themes (pressure, job security, learning fatigue) add up to just 38 mentions. People have twice as much to say about what's working than what's not.
